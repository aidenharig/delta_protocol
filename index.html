The Rebound Protocol

A 14-week return-from-illness program built to recover lost ground, then surpass it. Designed for symptom-gated execution off post-mononucleosis recovery, with hypertrophy as the principal driver and tactical capacity as the long horizon.

01 // Doctrine

What this program actually is

Read this once. It governs every decision downstream.

Three weeks of forced rest from mononucleosis cost you almost no muscle. Myonuclei persist for months. What you actually lost is contractile efficiency, neural drive, and aerobic capacity — all of which respond to any progressive stimulus and return faster than first acquired. The "muscle memory" effect isn't folklore; it's the dominant signal of weeks 3–6.

What can still hurt you is invisible. Mono leaves cellular debris in muscle tissue, can transiently enlarge the spleen, disrupts deep-stage sleep architecture for weeks after symptomatic recovery, and tanks ferritin. Train through any of those and you don't just stall — you risk rolling the illness back out. The rate limiter on this program is not training; it is the recovery substrate underneath it.

First Principle

Hypertrophy is driven by mechanical tension at proximity to failure, not by maximum load. You can grow muscle at 60% 1RM if you take it close to failure. This is the unlock that lets the rebound be both cautious and productive at the same time. Light loads, hard sets, no compromise on results.

The summer is structured in four phases over fourteen weeks, gated by symptom response — not by calendar. You move forward when the body confirms it's ready. The program supports two execution paths: a Conservative ramp if your doctor finds anything ongoing, and a Standard ramp if you're fully cleared. Both end in the same place by week 14.

Background The science behind the doctrine

Detraining of 21–28 days: ~5–10% drop in 1RM strength, <3% drop in muscle cross-sectional area, ~10–15% drop in VO2 max. Strength returns within 2–4 weeks of resumed training; aerobic returns within 4–6 weeks of resumed volume.

Post-EBV physiology: Even after symptom resolution, lymphocytic infiltration of skeletal muscle is documented for 4–8 weeks. NREM sleep can stay disrupted 8+ weeks. Iron metabolism can stay impaired for months. None of this prevents training — it dictates its volume and intensity envelope.

Why hypertrophy first, strength second: Strength expression depends on neural drive, intramuscular coordination, and CNS readiness. All three are downstream of recovery quality. Hypertrophy is more forgiving of suboptimal recovery — you can drive growth on a so-so day. You can't drive a true 1RM PR on one. Building the muscle first creates the substrate the strength block then maps onto.

Concurrent endurance, why now: The literature on AMPK/mTOR interference is real but graded. The interference is dose-dependent and modality-dependent. Cycling interferes less than running due to lower eccentric load and lower CNS cost. Sub-45-minute Zone 2 sessions, 2–3x per week, post-lift or on off-days, sit safely below the interference threshold.

02 // Clearance Gate

Before anything else

The conversation with your doctor sets the path. Walk in with this list — most appointments don't cover these by default.

At your clearance appointment, ask explicitly
"Beyond general activity, what level of load am I cleared for?"
  1. Spleen status. Has splenomegaly fully resolved? If unsure, request an ultrasound. Heavy compound lifts at high intensity create Valsalva pressure that has been associated with splenic rupture in athletes still in recovery — this is the single non-negotiable gate.
  2. Heavy Valsalva clearance. Specifically: am I cleared for back squats, deadlifts, and overhead presses at intensities above 80% of pre-illness training maxes?
  3. Bloodwork. Request ferritin, full CBC, vitamin D, and TSH. These are not standard mono follow-up but are the four most likely sub-clinical issues left over from EBV that will silently sabotage training. Ask for the actual numbers, not just "normal."
  4. Residual fatigue check. Be honest about energy, sleep quality, and any recurring sore throat or lymph node tenderness. These determine which path below you start on.
If incomplete recovery
Conservative Path
Phase 0 extended to 2 weeks. Phase 1 starts at 50% load. Endurance held to walking only until week 3. Re-evaluate weekly.
If fully cleared
Standard Path
Phase 0 compressed to 1 week. Phase 1 starts at 60% load. Bike Zone 2 introduced week 2. Standard 14-week build.
Symptom Flare Protocol

If at any point you experience: recurring sore throat, swollen lymph nodes, fatigue lasting >48 hr after a session, low-grade fever, or upper-left abdominal discomfort — stop immediately. Drop back one phase. If the symptom persists 72 hr, return to clinic. This is the single rule that overrides every other rule in this document.

Why one set

A single heavy set drives neural adaptation without the systemic cost of a strength block. The hypertrophy volume that follows is what builds the muscle mass that lets the strength climb. This is the most efficient strength reintegration coming off detraining — and it's the reason the program ends with a 5RM test, not a 1RM.

Volume drops 40%. You test 5RMs, not maxes. Compare to projected 5RM from your pre-mono 1RMs (1RM × 0.87 ≈ 5RM). If you match those numbers, the rebound succeeded. If you exceed them, the program built net new capacity on top of the rebound — exactly the goal.

05 // Endurance Layer

The cardio that won't fight your gains

Bike-led endurance with strategic running. The volume is calibrated to sit beneath the AMPK/mTOR interference threshold while still developing aerobic base for the long-horizon tactical goal.

Modality Why bike, not run, for the rebound

Cycling is a closed-chain, low-eccentric movement. Running is a high-eccentric impact loaded movement. The difference matters for two reasons:

  • Eccentric loading drives muscle damage that competes with hypertrophy recovery. Cycling produces a fraction of this.
  • Impact loading stresses joints already deconditioned by 3+ weeks off. Running too early in the rebound is the most common cause of overuse injury in returning athletes.

Running gets reintroduced in Phase 2 as short tempo work, then again in Phase 3 as occasional aerobic. Long runs stay off the menu through week 14 — they're the modality that most threatens the hypertrophy goal.

06 // Mobility & Movement

Maintain the chassis

Your existing FRC-based CARs routine, McGill core foundation, and knee/lower back accommodations carry forward intact. The protocol is unchanged from the April program.

AM Morning CARs Routine — 10 min daily

Done first thing, every morning. Slow controlled rotations through full range of each joint. Two reps each direction.

  1. Neck CARs (slow, no force) — 2/direction
  2. Shoulder CARs (each side) — 2/direction
  3. Spine segmental — cat/cow then thoracic rotation 5/side
  4. Hip CARs (each side) — 2/direction
  5. Knee CARs — 2/direction each leg
  6. Ankle CARs — 5/direction each foot
  7. 90/90 hip switches — 6 reps
  8. Wall ankle dorsiflexion — 5/side
Core McGill Big 3 — every lift day, integrated

Anti-movement framework: anti-extension, anti-rotation, anti-lateral-flexion. Done at the end of the lift session. Progressive overload across each phase.

  1. McGill Curl-Up: 3 holds at 8 sec each. One leg straight, one bent. Hands under lower back. Lift head/shoulders just off floor. (Switch leg position halfway through the holds — this is one continuous block, not "each side.")
  2. Side Plank: 3 x 30 sec each side. Phase 2+: progress to 45 sec.
  3. Bird-Dog: 3 x 8 reps each side. Hold each rep 2 sec at full extension.

Lower back accommodations woven in: Pallof presses on Lower B days, single-leg glute bridges on warm-ups, banded lateral walks before any squat session.

Knees Knee accommodations
  • Terminal knee extensions (band) — 2 x 15 each leg before any quad-dominant day
  • Wall ankle dorsiflexion mobility — 5/side daily
  • Slow eccentric on every quad movement (3 sec down minimum)
  • Avoid leg press at >90° hip flexion if knee tightness shows up
  • Copenhagen plank progression — 2 x 20 sec/side, 2x/week
Pre-lift Session-specific dynamic warm-ups

10–12 min, tailored to the day. The warm-up is the bridge between the cold body that walked into the gym and the body that can express force safely.

  • World's greatest stretch 5/side
  • Banded lateral walks 10/direction
  • Bodyweight squat 10 reps slow
  • 2 warm-up sets of the day's first lift
Push / upper days
  • 3 min bike or row
  • Wall slides 10 reps
  • Band pull-aparts 15 reps
  • Scapular push-ups 10 reps
  • Arm circles, both directions, 10 each
  • 2 warm-up sets of the day's first lift

Critical Iron & micronutrient priorities

Mono frequently leaves ferritin low. Low ferritin sabotages endurance training and can produce fatigue indistinguishable from residual mono. Address it deliberately:

  • Heme iron sources daily: red meat 2x/week minimum, dark poultry, sardines (you already eat these)
  • Vitamin C with iron-rich meals: citrus, bell pepper, strawberry — increases absorption ~3x
  • Avoid coffee/tea within 1 hr of iron-rich meals — tannins block absorption
  • Re-test ferritin at week 6. If still low (<50 ng/mL for an athlete), supplement under doctor guidance

Other micros to watch: vitamin D (3,000 IU/day continues), magnesium (400mg evening, supports sleep + recovery), zinc (15mg, immune + testosterone). All cheap, all relevant.

Stack Supplement protocol
  • Creatine monohydrate — 5g daily, anytime, with water. Continue uninterrupted.
  • Whey protein — as needed to hit protein target, 1–2 servings/day
  • Vitamin D3 — 3,000 IU/day with a fatty meal
  • Magnesium glycinate — 400mg, 30 min before bed
  • Omega-3s — 2g EPA+DHA via canned sardines, salmon, or fish oil
  • Zinc — 15mg, 2–3x/week, not daily

Hold on: aggressive pre-workout stacks. Caffeine is fine; the Celsius + pre-workout stack you flagged previously stays off the table during P0–P1. Reintroduce moderate caffeine (one source per session) in P2 if needed.

Hydration Hydration protocol — carries over

Existing protocol from the April block stands. LMNT electrolyte packets, day-type-specific targets:

  • Lift days: ~1 gallon water + 1 LMNT
  • Bike/run days: ~1.25 gal + 1–2 LMNT
  • Rest days: ~3L water
  • Sauna days: +500mL + 1 LMNT post-sauna
08 // Sleep & Recovery

The actual rate limiter

Mono specifically disrupts deep NREM sleep — the stage where 70%+ of growth hormone release happens. Without restored sleep architecture, the entire training stimulus is wasted. This is the single highest-leverage protocol in the document.

Priority 1 Sleep protocol — non-negotiable
  1. Fixed wake time, 7 days/week. Within 30 min, regardless of when you fell asleep. Anchors the circadian system.
  2. Morning light, 5–10 min, within 30 min of waking. Outside if possible. Not through a window.
  3. Last meal 3 hr before bed minimum. Late eating wrecks deep sleep architecture.
  4. No caffeine after 2pm. Half-life 5–6 hr — afternoon caffeine still in your system at midnight.
  5. Cool room (65–68°F). Body temp drop is the primary deep sleep trigger.
  6. No screens in bed. If awake and unable to sleep, leave the bed. Bed = sleep, only.
  7. Magnesium glycinate 400mg, 30 min before bed. Modest but real effect on sleep onset and depth.
  8. Target 8 hr in bed, not 8 hr asleep. Real sleep efficiency is 85–90% — plan accordingly.
Recovery Sauna protocol — carries over

Existing 4x/week protocol holds: 15–20 min at 80–100°C, post-lift sessions and Sunday. Hydration replenishment afterward as protocoled. The cardiovascular and growth hormone benefits are well-documented and synergistic with the rebound.

Recovery Active recovery & off-day movement
  • Walk 20–30 min after dinner — circadian + glucose disposal
  • Foam roll major muscle groups 5–10 min, 3x/week
  • One full rest day per week — no structured activity, walking only
  • Optional: cold shower 1–2 min at end of regular shower (NOT post-lift on muscle-building days — cold blunts hypertrophy signaling for ~6 hr)
Tracking Recovery markers worth tracking

Don't track everything. Track these four:

  1. Resting HR upon waking — >5 bpm above baseline for 2+ days = under-recovered
  2. Subjective energy 1–10 — log first thing in the morning
  3. Sleep duration — under 7hr for 2 consecutive nights = throttle the next session
  4. Bodyweight — weekly only, same day, same time
09 // Mind & Environment

The other half of the operator

You came into this program after illness. Coming out of it, you're a college junior with a startup. The cognitive load is real and the program won't survive it without environmental design.

Mental Pre-session mental shift — carries over

The existing protocol from April applies. Cognitive fatigue inflates RPE, which causes you to under-perform what your body could actually do. The shift isn't motivational — it's a state change.

  1. Close laptop. Phone face-down. Stand up.
  2. 3 minutes of nasal breathing, 4-second in, 6-second out
  3. One sentence written down: what am I training today?
  4. Walk to the gym, no podcast/music for the first 5 min — let the mind decompress
  5. Music on at the door
Critical Honest weekly check-in

Sunday evening, 5 minutes. Write down answers, don't just think them.

  1. Energy this week, 1–10. Lower than 6 = phase pause next week.
  2. Did anything mono-adjacent show up? Sore throat, swollen glands, fatigue spike, low fever?
  3. Sleep average — ballpark hours per night.
  4. Mood — any persistent low? Mono lies, but so does post-illness depression. Both real.
  5. Was a session skipped? Why? Honestly.
The point

This is the single most important habit in the program. The Outsider on the council flagged it — "are you still recovering or are you actually back?" — and the answer can only come from honest weekly self-audit, not from how the lifts feel in the moment.

Environment Cognitive load & startup management
  • One hard gym slot per day, locked. Same time, same calendar block. Decisions about when to train consume executive function — eliminate them.
  • Pre-pack gym bag the night before. Friction is the silent killer of programs.
  • No startup work after 9pm. Cortisol stays elevated for hours after intense cognitive work; deep sleep depends on its decline.
  • One full half-day off per week. No school, no startup, no training planning. Required, not optional.
  • Identify your two highest-leverage school deadlines for any given week and protect the morning before each one — no early gym, full sleep priority.
Identity A note on the long horizon

The original Delta-tier resources are still on the table. This summer is not that — and it shouldn't try to be. The fastest path to elite tactical capacity from where you are now is, paradoxically, a hypertrophy and base-building summer. Every operator-level program assumes a strength and aerobic foundation already exists. The summer's job is to build that foundation back, then surpass it. Special-ops-style work picks up cleanly in the fall, against a body that can take it.

Be patient with the velocity. Be impatient with the destination.

Stop When to abort the program

Stop and re-clinic if any of these occur:

  • Recurring sore throat or swollen lymph nodes (any time)
  • Fatigue lasting >48 hr after a session, twice in one phase
  • Upper-left abdominal pain or discomfort
  • Low-grade fever during a training week
  • Sleep quality drops persistently for >7 nights
  • Resting HR >7 bpm above baseline for >5 days

None of these mean the program failed. They mean recovery is incomplete and the program needs to wait. Aborting is data, not weakness.
